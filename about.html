About me

Hi, I'm Naomi.
You already know what I do —
and honestly, that's a relief.

If you're here, you probably already know what a UX designer does. I spend a lot of time explaining it to people who think I make logos. So it's nice to skip that part and just talk about the work.

The name

Why Curiosity Lab?

Because the best work I've ever done started with a question I wasn't supposed to ask. Curiosity is how I find the thing underneath the thing — the real friction, the actual need, the decision nobody thought to document.

"This portfolio is built the same way: not a gallery, but a lab. Things get tested here. Some of them ship."

The name is also a personal commitment. Staying curious is how I stay honest about what users actually need versus what we think they need.

How I think

UX fit my operating system before I knew what UX was.

I came to design the long way — through art, film, pattern recognition, and a lifelong habit of noticing how systems connect. My first grade teacher caught me trying to draw in three-point perspective. I didn't have the vocabulary for it yet, but I was already trying to understand how things relate to each other in space.

That instinct didn't go away. It just found a better home in product design. I think in flows, not screens. In cause and effect, not components. In the whole map, not the current pin. Systems thinking isn't a methodology I learned — it's closer to a factory setting.

Design philosophy

Listen first. Not to be polite — because the real problem is almost never the one in the brief.

I've changed export formats because a customer told me she shouldn't have to pay for another subscription just to read her own data. I've rebuilt dashboards because the people using them couldn't see what they actually needed to see.

Relating to people isn't soft skill filler for me. It's the method. Better products come from better listening, and better listening comes from genuinely caring what someone's Tuesday afternoon feels like when the tool isn't working.

Outside the work

I make things that don't have user flows.

I'm currently writing a conceptual art book of poems — structured in three parts, designed like an album, meant to be experienced as a journey rather than read as a collection. It's the most nonlinear thing I make, which probably says something about how I think about everything else.

Let's talk

Open to new roles.

I'm looking for a team that cares about building things that actually work for the people using them. If that sounds like yours, I'd love to connect.
