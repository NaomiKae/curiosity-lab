Case Study 01  ·  Service Design

Southwest Airlines COS Policy Redesign

A policy exists. A process does not — and the gap is costing passengers their dignity.

Role UX & Service Designer
Methods Service Blueprint, Journey Mapping
Focus Equity + Accessibility
Status Concept Redesign
The 55-second version
The problem
Southwest has a Customer of Size policy but no consistent process to enforce it with dignity. The result is a gate confrontation — made in public, under pressure, with no private alternative — that humiliates passengers and varies trip to trip.
What I designed
Six upstream interventions that eliminate the gate moment entirely: a standardized size metric, proactive policy communication, booking-time COS option, reserved paired seats, a kiosk check-in alert, and a discreet boarding pass symbol.
The key insight
The gate confrontation is not where the system fails. It's where the failure finally surfaces. Every stage upstream — booking, check-in, boarding — failed to create a dignified alternative. Fix those, and the gate moment disappears.
The outcome
A redesign that requires no new employee training, no airport infrastructure changes, and no policy overhaul. The system does the work that's currently left to individual human judgment under pressure.
The Problem

A policy exists. A process does not.

Southwest Airlines has a Customer of Size (COS) policy — but it has no standardized process for enforcing it with consistency or dignity. The result is a system that depends entirely on one gate agent's judgment call, made in public, under pressure, with no private alternative available to the passenger.

This is not a training problem. It is a design problem. And it is entirely solvable.

Current state
  • Policy enforcement is inconsistent — same body, different outcome
  • No system flag from booking through check-in
  • Passenger is surprised at the gate, in public
  • Loses boarding group and seat choice
  • May be separated from travel companions
  • Flight attendant reseats publicly mid-boarding
  • Financial barrier — second seat at full fare price
  • No dignified alternative exists at any touchpoint
Future state
  • Objective size metric removes judgment from the equation
  • COS option surfaced at booking with transparent pricing
  • Paired seats reserved and guaranteed in the system
  • Kiosk alert at check-in — private, adjacent to counter staff
  • Discreet boarding pass symbol — readable by staff only
  • Pre-boards with families — no announcement, no spectacle
  • Seatbelt extender handed privately at the door
  • No new employee training required
Research + Discovery

The failure starts long before the gate.

Mapping the current state service blueprint revealed something critical: the gate confrontation — the most visible and humiliating moment in this experience — is not where the system fails. It is where the failure finally surfaces. The damage is done across every stage that precedes it.

The pre-flight and check-in stages were mapped from policy documentation and customer service records. The gate, boarding, and in-flight stages are grounded in real customer accounts documented publicly on social media.

Pre-flight
Missed signal

Policy exists but offers no clarity

Customers research a vague, clinical policy page that doesn't describe what will actually happen. Many call customer service and still leave without a clear answer. Those who can afford a second seat buy one with no guarantee of consistent treatment. Those who cannot — don't travel at all.

Check-in
Missed opportunity

Two touchpoints. Zero intervention.

Both the outdoor kiosk and indoor counter pass the customer through without mention of the policy. No system flag, no private conversation, no opportunity to purchase a second seat calmly. The customer clears security with a false sense of security — now trapped with no way to rebook.

Gate
Public crisis

The judgment call — made in front of everyone

At the boarding scan, a gate agent decides — with no script, no standard, and a full audience — whether this passenger needs a second seat. Enforcement varies between legs of the same trip. The passenger loses their boarding group, their seat choice, and their composure.

⬡ Documented customer account · Social media
Boarding
Dignity violation

Walked up and down a full plane

Assigned to the last row after boarding last. Asked to navigate past seated passengers. Emergency exit row restrictions enforced reactively. Flight attendants improvise reseating with no protocol and a departure delay clock running. Every other passenger watches.

⬡ Documented customer account · Social media
In-flight
Emotional harm

The harm travels with the customer at 30,000 feet

Seated alone in the back row, separated from companions, spending the entire flight holding back tears. Waiting to be the last passenger off the plane so she can find a private space to cry. The flight ends. The harm does not.

⬡ Documented customer account · Social media
"I spent the whole flight trying not to break down — and ran to the bathroom to cry."

This is an equity and access issue — not just a customer experience one

The policy creates a two-tiered outcome: passengers who can afford a surprise second seat fare face humiliation. Passengers who cannot afford it face exclusion. For many, the journey ends before it begins — not because of their body, but because of a system that never anticipated their needs.

The Design Solution

Handle everything upstream so nothing needs to be handled in public.

The redesign does not require new employee training, new airport infrastructure, or a policy overhaul. It requires the system to do the work that is currently being left to individual human judgment under pressure. Six interventions, all upstream, eliminate the gate confrontation entirely.

01
Safety + Compliance

Standardized size metric

Safety and compliance defines an objective, consistent standard — removing individual judgment from enforcement. Framed around comfort, distributed company-wide as an accessible infographic. Includes height accommodations for tall passengers.

02
Website

Proactive policy communication

Hero alert on the website homepage, persistent nav badge, and clear framing around passenger comfort — not around being a problem. Customers are informed before they book, in private, at home.

03
Booking flow

COS option with transparent pricing

Tooltip during ticket purchase with size chart reference. COS pricing tier shown upfront. Booking system automatically couples adjacent seat — reserved, guaranteed, no surprises. Passengers can budget effectively before they commit.

04
Seating system

Reserved paired seats

COS selection couples two adjacent seats in the system. Those seats are held — unavailable to other passengers. Open seating no longer puts the burden of seat choice on the passenger of size.

05
Check-in

Kiosk alert with discreet purchase option

For passengers who didn't pre-select COS, a gentle kiosk alert surfaces the option at a reduced rate — positioned adjacent to counter staff if assistance is needed. Private, calm, with time and space to decide.

06
Gate + Boarding

Discreet symbol, pre-board with families

Digital boarding pass marked with a staff-readable symbol. COS passengers pre-board with families — no announcement, no separate line, no spectacle. Seatbelt extender handed privately at the door. Seated before general boarding begins.

Core design principle

Remove judgment from the equation. The system handles it — not the agent, not the flight attendant, not the passenger.

Business case

No new employee training required at scale. No airport infrastructure changes. Implemented through booking system updates, digital pass logic, and website copy.

The Outcome

The same boarding experience for everyone.

The measure of success for this redesign is not a satisfaction score. It is the absence of an incident. No public conversations. No lost boarding positions. No reseating in front of a full plane. No waiting to be last off so you can cry in private.

The customer of size boards the plane the same way every other passenger does. They find their reserved seat. They settle in. The flight departs on time. It is unremarkable. That is the goal.

No judgment calls. No public conversations. No parading. No crying in airport bathrooms.

The plane doesn't end the harm. It contains it. This redesign ensures the harm never begins.

Reflections

What this project taught me about service design.

The most important insight from this case study is that the gate confrontation was never the problem — it was the symptom. Every stage upstream failed to create a dignified alternative, so the gate became the only intervention point. That's a systems failure, not a people failure.

Designing the solution meant resisting the urge to fix the symptom (train gate agents better) and instead mapping backward to the root causes: no consistent metric, no upstream communication, no system support. When you fix those, the gate moment fixes itself.

The symptom is never the problem. The gate confrontation was the most visible failure point — but fixing it in isolation would have been cosmetic. The real work was tracing backward to every missed signal upstream.
Equity and dignity are not constraints on good design — they are the measure of it. A system that works for the most vulnerable passengers works better for everyone.
The best design is unremarkable. The goal wasn't a better gate experience — it was no gate experience at all. When the system works, the passenger just boards the plane.

The same trip. A completely different experience.

What this redesign proves is that the harm was never inevitable. It was designed in — through vague policy, missed touchpoints, and a system that left the hardest moments to human improvisation. Design can undo that. Not with new training, not with new infrastructure, but by deciding that the system should handle what shouldn't be left to chance.

This project also reinforced that equity and dignity are not constraints on good design — they are the measure of it.
