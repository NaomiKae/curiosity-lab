Case Study 03  ·  Service Design

The AI-DLC Design Handoff

I didn't know where I fit. So I figured it out — and the whole company ended up using it.

Role UX Designer
Type Process Design · Service Design
Timeline Resolved in under 48 hours
Outcome Adopted company-wide + AWS training
The 55-second version
The problem
Our team adopted AI-DLC — AWS's AI-driven development process — but nobody defined where design fit. The FE developer had no visual source of truth. I had no clear moment to contribute. Construction started without either of us having what we needed.
What I built
A designer's checklist for the inception phase and a workflow diagram showing exactly where design gets injected between inception and construction — so the team always knows when a visual source of truth exists.
The signal
When the FE developer asked "what am I supposed to know what this looks like?" and nobody had an answer — that was the moment I knew the problem wasn't the process itself. It was a missing handoff that nobody had named.
The outcome
The Dev Director, QA Manager, PM, and Design Manager all validated the proposal the same day. Leadership adopted it company-wide. AWS facilitators incorporated it into mandatory training the following week.
The before state

A new process that moved fast and left design behind

Our organization adopted AI-DLC — Amazon's AI-Driven Development Lifecycle — a methodology that reimagines the full software development process around AI collaboration. The promise was significant: faster decisions, better artifacts, less rework. But the rollout was rough by everyone's admission, and for design specifically, there was a fundamental gap nobody had addressed.

The process had two major phases: Inception, where the whole team answers AI's questions to produce structured artifacts like user stories, units of work, and an application design document — and Construction, where the team builds from those artifacts. Somewhere between those two phases, the FE developer was supposed to have a visual source of truth. Nobody had defined who was supposed to create it or when.

"What am I supposed to know what this thing is supposed to look like?"

That question from our FE developer wasn't a complaint. It was a diagnosis. Construction had started, code was being written, and there was nothing visual to build toward. The PM and Dev Lead were tagging me in Slack asking if I could help. I didn't have an answer — because I was waiting for something to come out of the process that was supposed to tell me what to design. It never came.

Where the process broke down

Hours of questions. No visual output. No defined moment for design.

After researching the AI-DLC methodology in depth, I was able to map exactly where the breakdown was happening. The inception phase was producing structured text artifacts — and those artifacts were going directly to the PM after each section. I had no visibility into them, no defined moment to react to them, and no prompt to turn them into something visual before construction started.

The process was designed around engineering decisions. The design layer had been bolted on as an afterthought, with no defined handoff moment between "AI produces text artifacts" and "FE dev starts building."

1
Inception produced artifacts — but only the PM saw them
After hours of mob elaboration, the AI produced user stories, units of work, and an application design document. These went to the PM after each section. Design was not in the loop, had no defined moment to review them, and received no prompt to act on them before construction kicked off.
2
Construction started without a visual source of truth
The team moved into mob construction — synchronous, intensive build sessions — before any design artifact existed. The FE developer had no screens, no flows, no component reference. Questions started surfacing in Slack that couldn't be answered because the foundational visual work had never been done.
3
The missing step had a name — it just hadn't been defined
The inception artifacts are structured, detailed, and rich with context. They read like a PRD. I had already proven — on a parallel project I was running my own way — that I could take a PRD into Claude Design and produce wireframes fast enough for the team to build from. The gap wasn't a skill problem. It was a process problem. Nobody had defined the step between "inception closes" and "construction begins" where design does that translation.
I was doing it my usual way on the side and got to something the devs could build from way faster. That was the tell.
The solution

Name the step. Define the role. Give the team something to hold.

Once I understood the gap, the fix was straightforward — but it needed to be documented and made official. I produced two artifacts: a checklist of what design needs to bring into the inception phase, and a workflow diagram that makes the handoff moment visible for the whole team.

The core insight was this: the inception artifacts are the PRD. The designer's job is to take those artifacts into the appropriate design tool, treat them as a brief, and produce a visual artifact — at whatever fidelity the project needs — before construction starts. That output becomes the source of truth the FE developer has been asking for.

Deliverable 01
Inception phase checklist
What design needs to bring to mob elaboration — problem context, desired direction, design constraints, and the veto list. Structured as a shareable reference so nothing critical gets missed before the session.
Deliverable 02
Design handoff workflow
A visual diagram of the full AI-DLC flow with the design handoff moment clearly marked — showing where design takes inception artifacts into the design tool, produces a visual artifact, and gets it approved before construction begins.
The designer's job isn't to arrive with a solution. It's to arrive with taste, constraints, and a veto — and translate the artifacts into something the team can build from.

I posted both documents in the team Slack channel with a short note explaining where I thought the process had broken down and what I was proposing to fix it. I was careful to frame it as a solution to a shared problem — not as a correction or a complaint. The timing mattered. The team was actively blocked and had been asking me for something I couldn't provide. This was the answer.

The response

The Dev Director shared my post directly with my Design Manager.

Within hours of posting, the responses came in. My PM said I had taken the bull by the horns and confirmed the AI-DLC rollout had not been smooth — to put it mildly. The Dev Director said he liked it a lot and thought it was worth trying. The QA Manager noted that sister platforms had independently arrived at the same conclusion: wireframes between inception and construction is what works.

I had been nervous about my Design Manager's reaction — he and the CPO were actively working on a formal onboarding process for design within AI-DLC, and I was aware I might be seen as jumping ahead. But the Dev Director had already shared the post with him directly over Slack. His response came through shortly after.

"He really appreciated that and wanted me to know. I totally love it, too. Nice work!!"

What struck me most about that moment wasn't the validation — it was what it confirmed about the problem. The gap I had identified wasn't unique to our team. It was showing up across teams, across platforms, across organizations trying to adopt the same process. I hadn't invented a solution. I had named something that already needed to exist.

The outcome

Mandatory company-wide training. AWS facilitators. One week later.

Leadership adopted the workflow company-wide. The following week, the company held mandatory AWS training with facilitators from AWS — and my design handoff workflow was incorporated into the training materials. What started as me trying to unblock my own team became the answer the organization was looking for as it rolled out a new development methodology at scale.

The checklist and workflow are now the official reference for how design participates in AI-DLC inception and construction across teams. The FE developer has a source of truth before construction starts. Design has a defined moment and a defined role. The handoff that nobody named has a name.

What I learned

This is what service design looks like from the inside.

I didn't frame this as service design while I was doing it. I was just frustrated and trying to solve a problem. But looking back, that's exactly what this was: mapping a system, identifying where it broke down for the humans inside it, and designing a fix that made the whole flow work better for everyone.

The symptom is never the problem. The FE developer asking "what does this look like?" was the symptom. The problem was a missing handoff moment that nobody had named. Fixing the symptom would have meant producing one-off wireframes. Fixing the problem meant making the step official.
Your existing workflow is data. The fact that I was producing better, faster output by running my usual process on the side wasn't a workaround — it was evidence. That parallel track told me exactly what the AI-DLC process was missing and what the fix needed to look like.
Coming with solutions changes the conversation. I could have posted in Slack saying the process was broken. Instead I posted a workflow and a checklist. That reframe — from complaint to proposal — is what made the response land the way it did at every level of leadership.
You don't need to arrive with answers. You need to arrive with opinions. My role in inception isn't to pre-design the solution. It's to bring context, constraints, and the willingness to react fast when the AI proposes something. That's the whole job — and knowing that made the ambiguity manageable.

The process didn't just get a fix. It got a designer in the room.

What this project taught me is that design's value in AI-driven development isn't just in the pixels — it's in the decisions. The moment design gets access to the inception artifacts and a defined space to react to them, the whole team moves faster and builds with more confidence. That's the outcome I'm most proud of.

The company now has a process. Sister platforms independently confirmed it works. AWS is teaching it. And our FE developer has something to build from before she writes a single line of code.
